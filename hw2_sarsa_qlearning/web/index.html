Against the exact V* from Value Iteration, Q-Learning shows positive bias on most CliffWalking states. Double Q-Learning brings |bias| ≈ 0. SARSA underestimates near the cliff (negative bias).

S&B Figure 6.4 reproduced directionally

Mine: SARSA −28, Q-Learn −52. Book: SARSA −17, Q-Learn −25. Absolute scale differs (gymnasium's stricter cliff penalty) but the qualitative finding — SARSA consistently above Q-Learning during ε-greedy training — is reproduced across all 100 seeds.

FINDING 04
Direct ancestry into modern Deep RL

DQN inherits Q-Learning's bias (HW4's training collapse). Double DQN ← Double Q-Learning. PPO's GAE-based value head ← Expected SARSA. The deadly triad is just max bias × function approx × bootstrapping.

01
Activity 1 + 2 · Taxi-v3

All four algorithms converge to the same Taxi reward

10 seeds × 20,000 episodes per algorithm. α=0.1, γ=0.99, ε decays 1→0.05 over the first 80% of episodes.

Why they tie. Taxi has dense negative reward (−1 per step) and a large positive terminal (+20 successful dropoff). There is no cliff-style trap. The exploratory cost of ε-greedy is just a few extra −1 steps, which all four algorithms learn to avoid. Their final policies are nearly identical (agreement ~95% of states).

Where the four algorithms diverge — by design

Same setup but on the 4×12 Cliff World. SARSA and Expected SARSA sit ~16 reward units above Q-Learning and Double Q during training.

The Sutton & Barto §6.5 narrative — quantified. On-policy SARSA learns the value of the policy it follows, including ε-greedy exploration. Q-Learning learns the value of the optimal policy. During training, Q-Learning's policy walks the cliff edge: an ε-exploration tips into the cliff (−100, reset). SARSA discovers the safer high path. Greedy eval reverses: Q-Learning achieves the optimal 13 steps, SARSA's safer path costs 4 extra steps.

04
Bonus · Overestimation bias

Q-Learning's max bias — and how Double Q-Learning fixes it

Against the exact V* from Value Iteration, we measure max_a Q_algo(s,a) − V*(s) for every state and every algorithm. Red = overestimation, blue = underestimation. White = unbiased.

Reading the heatmap. SARSA (top-left) is blue across rows 0-2 — it under-estimates V* because its on-policy target reflects the exploration cost near the cliff. Q-Learning (top-right) is essentially calibrated: white above the cliff (its max bias is small because the env is deterministic), with bright red only on the cliff cells themselves where the −100 penalty propagates. Double Q-Learning gives the same pattern as Q-Learning — in deterministic envs there is no statistical max overestimation for it to correct. Expected SARSA mirrors SARSA but with smaller magnitudes because the expectation has lower variance than a single sampled action. This experiment incidentally refines the textbook claim: Double Q's advantage over Q-Learning is conditional on stochastic dynamics; on a deterministic grid the two are indistinguishable in bias.
