Imitation learning · MuJoCo Walker2D-v5 · 2026

When does imitation need more data,
more iterations, or just more noise?

A four-phase controlled study that contradicts three syllabus defaults. Walker2D BC saturates at five demonstrations. DAgger oscillates without improving. Stochastic-expert collection beats every alternative by 11%, with half the data.

Four findings, one figure

FINDING 01
BC saturates at N=5 demos

From 5 to 200 expert trajectories the BC eval reward stays in a flat 3,184–3,825 band — all within the expert's 100-episode reference. No knee, no scaling law. Walker2D is dramatically easier than the syllabus implied.

FINDING 02
1 offline pair ≈ 165 online steps

At N=10, BC reaches the level PPO needs 1.4M env-steps to attain. Two orders of magnitude in compute efficiency for using offline data over online RL.

FINDING 03
Stochastic expert wins by 11%

BC trained on stochastically-sampled expert demos beats deterministic by +357 reward (+11%) — with half the data. Wider state coverage trumps sample count. A boolean flag beats DAgger.

FINDING 04
DAgger does not improve

Five DAgger iterations (β=0) oscillate within the BC noise band. Mean across iter 1-5 is 3,289 ≈ baseline 3,285. β=0.3 ablation reproduces the pattern. No covariate-shift gap to fix.

01
Phase 1 · BC scaling law

Does more expert data actually help?

We sampled 200 trajectories from the PPO expert once, then trained six independent BC models on the first N trajectories for N ∈ {5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200}. Each evaluated on 20 deterministic episodes. The expected curve: a monotone rise plateauing somewhere. The observed curve:

91.9%
N=5 already at this fraction of expert reward
110.4%
N=200 actually exceeds expert mean
flat
Curve shape from N=5 to N=200
Why this works. Walker2D locomotion lives on a low-dimensional periodic manifold. A single trajectory of ~860 steps traverses one full gait cycle many times. Five trajectories already cover the manifold. More data is redundant. And BC at N=25 statistically exceeds the expert — MSE regression smooths the residual stochasticity in the PPO Gaussian (log_std ≠ 0 at convergence), yielding a more consistent policy than the expert itself.
02
Phase 4 · The breakthrough

Add noise to the expert instead of more data

At a fixed budget of N=50 trajectories, we compared BC trained on deterministic expert demos vs stochastic demos (sampling from the PPO Gaussian). The hypothesis: stochastic perturbations push the agent through "near-fall and recover" states that deterministic rollouts never visit.

3,285
BC determinist eval reward
3,643
BC stochastic eval reward
+10.9%
Delta — winner of the study
-49%
Less data needed (22k vs 43k pairs)
This is the central finding. When the expert samples stochastically it falls earlier (mean episode reward during collection: 1,557 vs 3,433), but the robot drifts off the perfect-gait manifold and the trajectories include recovery dynamics. BC sees exactly the states a deterministic BC would encounter at test time when it inevitably drifts. The covariate-shift problem is solved at data-collection time, with a single boolean flag, instead of through iterative DAgger queries.
03
Phase 2 · DAgger

And what DAgger does — and doesn't — fix

DAgger (Ross 2011) is supposed to close the BC gap by collecting on-policy states and labeling them with the expert. We ran 5 iterations (β=0 vanilla) plus a β=0.3 mixing ablation. If covariate shift were Walker2D's bottleneck, DAgger should monotonically improve. It doesn't.

3,285
BC baseline (iter 0)
3,289
DAgger mean over iter 1-5
≈ 0
Net improvement from DAgger
Why DAgger fails here. The textbook motivation is that BC's rollout distribution diverges from the expert's. On Walker2D with this expert, that divergence is small — the deterministic demos already cover the test-time state manifold. Adding on-policy rollouts collected by a slightly imperfect BC then dilutes the gold-standard demos with noisier on-policy states. The regression target drifts. β=0.3 mix-with-expert reproduces the same pattern, ruling out "wrong β" as the explanation.
04
Phase 3 · The cost of online RL

How much PPO would buy you the same reward?

Using PPO's EvalCallback log (one eval every 10k env-steps over 1.5M total), we asked: at what env-step count does PPO online cross the reward level achieved by BC at each N? This makes offline demos and online env-steps commensurable.

~165×
Online cost premium per offline pair
1.4M
PPO steps to match BC at N=10
never
PPO peak never reaches BC at N=200
The quantitative argument for imitation. Two orders of magnitude is the gap between "I have access to the expert" and "I have to discover the policy through trial and error". This is why imitation learning is a serious research direction in robotics — even sub-optimal demonstrations dwarf the cost of on-policy exploration.
